# CragCast Crag Guide

Domain knowledge behind the scores. This is the reasoning that sets each crag's metadata; use it when adding or recalibrating crags.

## The two axes, restated for the PNW
- **Steepness** decides whether rain sheds off or tracks in and seeps. Overhung rock stays climbable *through* rain and comes back first; low-angle slab holds water and seeps longest.
- **Aspect/sun** warms the rock surface past the dew point (burning off overnight condensation) — a friend on a cold damp morning, an enemy that bakes and greases holds on a hot afternoon. Each crag carries a compass **aspect** (the direction it faces), which drives the per-crag sun-exposure timeline; multi-aspect clusters (columns, spires, scattered mini-crags) are marked **varied**.
- **Rock type** sets drainage: rain-shadow basalt drains in hours; valley granite seeps for days.
- **Elevation/exposure** sets lag after weather and wind exposure.

## Areas

### Index — Granite, Skykomish Valley, WA (47.8207, −121.5720)
Low in a treed river valley: high ambient humidity at the base, notorious internal seepage, but genuine south sun that turns the walls on as the day warms. The most dew-point-sensitive granite in the region.
- **Lower Town Wall** — S-facing, low-angle→vertical. Greasy at dawn from valley humidity; morning sun burns it off. Great Northern Slab seeps longest; the steep central lines (Godzilla, City Park) come in first. `steep .45 · sun .8 · drain .28 · lagH 2 · aspect S`
- **Upper Town Wall · Cheeks** — S-facing but steep/overhung, the **rain refuge**: stays dry through actual rain and comes back first. Top-ropeable; the go-to when the rest of Index is seeping. `steep .92 · sun .72 · drain .35 · lagH 4 · aspect S`
- **Upper Town Wall** — S-facing multipitch main wall (Town Crier, Green Dragon). Less than vertical and tops onto a sloping hillside, so it takes rain and **seeps for days**. Higher and committing — a prime-stable-spell objective, not a marginal-day play. `steep .5 · sun .75 · drain .25 · lagH 6 · aspect S`
- **Lookout Point** — E-facing slab: drains fast but only catches morning sun, so it's quick to dry and slow to warm. Needs sun on it before friction shows. `steep .2 · sun .4 · drain .6 · lagH 2 · aspect E`

### Squamish — Granite, Sea-to-Sky, BC (49.6870, −123.1400)
Coastal granite; wetter and more maritime than Index, but bigger and with more aspect variety.
- **The Chief · Grand Wall** — W/SW-facing, steep; sheds better than the Apron, afternoon sun brings it around late. `steep .7 · sun .7 · drain .35 · lagH 3 · aspect WSW`
- **The Apron** — SW-facing low-angle friction slab, the most dew-point-critical terrain here; damp air alone shuts it down. Wants cold, crisp, low-humidity air. `steep .18 · sun .65 · drain .4 · lagH 2 · aspect SW`
- **Smoke Bluffs** — short walls, fast access, **varied aspects**; the pragmatic marginal-day/busy-day pick. `steep .5 · sun .6 · drain .45 · lagH 1 · aspect varied`

### Skaha Bluffs — Gneiss, Okanagan, BC (49.4387, −119.5506)
Semi-arid Okanagan sport cragging on coarse gneiss above Skaha Lake: hot, dry, sunny, and quick to dry after the rare rain. Aspect is everything for time-of-day comfort — parallel canyons give both morning (east) and afternoon (west) walls.
- **Great White Wall** — SW-facing steep gneiss, the marquee wall; sun-baked and greasy on a hot afternoon, comes around late. `steep .55 · sun .85 · drain .8 · lagH 1 · aspect SW`
- **Red Tail Wall** — E-facing: morning sun then afternoon shade, the hot-day pick once the sun swings behind it. `steep .45 · sun .6 · drain .8 · lagH 1 · aspect E`
- **Doctor's Wall** — the cooler, shadier corner (Fern Gully stays cool all day), the summer-heat refuge. `steep .5 · sun .5 · drain .8 · lagH 1 · aspect NE`

### The Bugaboos — Alpine granite, Purcell Mountains, BC (50.7383, −116.7633)
High glaciated granite spires (~2,300–3,200 m). Not cragging — the model treats it as alpine: a multi-day dry, warm window is the whole game, verglas and snow linger for days after a storm, and everything is exposed and committing. Big `lagH` reflects the slow dry-out.
- **Snowpatch Spire** — big multi-face spire, **varied aspect**; sun and light wind dry and warm the rock, a real system shuts it down. `steep .7 · sun .55 · drain .55 · lagH 10 · aspect varied`
- **Bugaboo Spire** — the Kain Route (NE ridge, 5.7); NE aspect is slow to warm and holds moisture, wants settled high pressure. `steep .6 · sun .5 · drain .55 · lagH 10 · aspect NE`
- **Pigeon Spire** — the West Ridge (5.4) knife-edge; W-facing rock dries in afternoon sun but is fully wind-exposed. `steep .55 · sun .6 · drain .6 · lagH 9 · aspect W`

### The Exits — Volcanic, I-90 / North Bend, WA (47.4419, −121.6556)
Low, treed, mossy, and the local drizzle-day refuge because the overhung crags stay dry.
- **Exit 38 · Far Side** — S-facing (nearly all the Far Side crags share southern exposure) and steep/overhung, the classic **rain bailout**: dry when everything west is dripping. `steep .9 · sun .5 · drain .4 · lagH 1 · aspect S`
- **Exit 32 · Little Si** — E-facing World-Wall terrain: catches morning sun, then goes shady around noon — the summer-afternoon pick. Steep, short approach, stays dry in light rain. `steep .72 · sun .45 · drain .4 · lagH 1 · aspect E`
- **Deception Crags** — lower-angle, heavily treed, **varied aspect** (scattered mini-crags facing different ways); holds moisture, prime-day-only. `steep .4 · sun .4 · drain .35 · lagH 2 · aspect varied`

### Leavenworth — Granite, east-slope Cascades, WA (47.5560, −120.6800)
East of the crest: drier and sunnier than the west-side crags, on coarse granite. The mega-classics are mostly sunny south/east faces that bake in summer — best in cool, dry spring and fall.
- **Snow Creek Wall** — the 800-ft premier wall, SE-facing high-friction granite; morning sun, bakes June–August, friction slab wants a low dew point. `steep .5 · sun .8 · drain .5 · lagH 3 · aspect SE`
- **Castle Rock** — the Tumwater Canyon roadside classic, W/SW-facing; comes around in the afternoon and dries fast in the dry air. `steep .55 · sun .7 · drain .55 · lagH 1 · aspect SW`
- **Careno Crag** — S-facing with little shade, low in Icicle Creek; bakes hot, comes into season early. `steep .5 · sun .85 · drain .55 · lagH 1 · aspect S`

### Vantage / Frenchman Coulee — Basalt, WA (46.9536, −119.9847)
East of the Cascades in the rain shadow: dries in hours, so it's the go-to when the west side is soaked. Full sun bakes it; summer friction is gone by midday. Wind is a real factor.
- **Sunshine Wall** — S/SW-facing vertical columns, sun-baked; PRIME after rain, an oven at midday in summer. `steep .6 · sun .95 · drain .95 · lagH 0 · aspect SSW`
- **The Feathers** — freestanding columns (**routes on the N and S sides → varied aspect**), dry almost instantly, but catch the full coulee wind (great as a breeze, miserable as a gale). `steep .55 · sun .85 · drain .95 · lagH 0 · aspect varied`
- **Middle Coulee** — N-facing (the Middle East Wall) shadier basalt, the hot-afternoon shade option. `steep .5 · sun .45 · drain .9 · lagH 0 · aspect N`

### Smith Rock — Welded Tuff, Central Oregon (44.3676, −121.1387)
High-desert rain shadow: dries fast and takes a lot of sun, but the soft welded tuff is fragile when wet — never climb it wet. Friction is best on cool mornings before the walls bake.
- **The Dihedrals** — SE-facing sport testpieces, sunny and steep; cool-morning friction, greasy by a hot afternoon. `steep .6 · sun .8 · drain .7 · lagH 1 · aspect SE`
- **Morning Glory Wall** — E-facing (the name earns it): morning sun turns the pockets on early, shade by afternoon. `steep .62 · sun .7 · drain .7 · lagH 1 · aspect E`
- **Christian Brothers · The Monument** — tall spires, **varied aspect** (E and W sides), more exposed and wind-affected; the slabbier lines want sun on them first. `steep .5 · sun .6 · drain .68 · lagH 2 · aspect varied`

### Broughton Bluff — Basalt, Columbia Gorge, OR (45.5386, −122.3746)
Gorge basalt at the wet mouth of the Columbia — mossy and seepier than desert basalt despite the rock; the steeper walls shed the worst of the rain.
- **Hanging Gardens** — SW-facing broken columns with cracks; the featured lines dry first. `steep .5 · sun .5 · drain .45 · lagH 1 · aspect SW`
- **North Face** — N-facing, taller (2–3 pitch): the shadiest, slowest-drying wall here; best on a dry, breezy spell, not right after weather. `steep .55 · sun .35 · drain .42 · lagH 2 · aspect N`
- **Red Wall · Trinity** — SW-facing friction slab and thin face, the most dew-point-sensitive terrain at Broughton; wants sun and crisp air. `steep .45 · sun .55 · drain .45 · lagH 1 · aspect SW`

### Trout Creek — Basalt columns, Deschutes Canyon, OR (44.8059, −121.1149)
South-facing columnar-basalt splitters in the Central Oregon rain shadow: drains in hours, full sun, a cool-season crack destination that bakes by midday in summer. **Closed Jan 15–Aug 31 for golden-eagle nesting.**
- **Main Wall** — S-facing splitters, full sun; drains fast. `steep .5 · sun .85 · drain .9 · lagH 0 · aspect S`
- **Castle Wall** — S/SW-facing, a touch more exposed to the canyon wind. `steep .55 · sun .8 · drain .9 · lagH 0 · aspect SSW`
- **Cool Wall** — N-facing shade option for a hot afternoon; same fast-draining basalt. `steep .5 · sun .5 · drain .9 · lagH 0 · aspect N`

### Yosemite Valley — Granite, Sierra Nevada, CA (37.7350, −119.6000)
Iconic Valley granite (~1,200 m). Low-humidity Sierra air dries rock fast, so heat and aspect matter more than wet: the sunny south faces bake while the big north faces stay cold and slow to dry.
- **El Capitan** — S-facing 3,000-ft monolith; the SW/SE faces bake, so heat is the enemy — chase shade or start pre-dawn in summer. `steep .7 · sun .8 · drain .4 · lagH 3 · aspect S`
- **Sentinel Rock** — mostly N-facing, cool and slow to dry (not in shape before June, best Aug–Sept); a summer shade refuge that stays damp after weather. `steep .6 · sun .35 · drain .35 · lagH 4 · aspect N`
- **Manure Pile Buttress** — S-facing roadside cragging (After Six, Nutcracker), quick to dry; the go-to when the north walls are seeping, bakes on a hot afternoon. `steep .45 · sun .75 · drain .45 · lagH 1 · aspect S`

### Tuolumne Meadows — Granite, Yosemite High Country, CA (37.8700, −119.3900)
High-country domes (~2,600 m): thin, dry Sierra air dries rock fast, but altitude means cold, a short summer season, and afternoon thunderstorms. Slabby friction is dew-point sensitive; sun warms the rock and turns the friction on.
- **Stately Pleasure Dome** — SW-facing roadside slab above Tenaya Lake; dries fast but greasy until the sun's on it (dew-point sensitive). `steep .35 · sun .8 · drain .6 · lagH 1 · aspect SW`
- **Fairview Dome** — 900-ft N-facing dome, cool and slow to warm — a hot-afternoon gift but slow to come back after weather. `steep .55 · sun .45 · drain .55 · lagH 3 · aspect N`
- **Cathedral Peak** — SE-buttress alpine spire (~3,300 m); exposed, so wind and afternoon storms are the hazard; SE catches morning sun and dries quickly. `steep .5 · sun .6 · drain .6 · lagH 4 · aspect SE`

### Joshua Tree — Monzogranite, Mojave Desert, CA (34.0117, −116.1666)
High-desert monzogranite that dries the instant rain stops — heat, not wet, sets the season (prime fall–spring, brutal summers). Coarse, grippy rock; you chase sun or shade by aspect.
- **Intersection Rock** — the 150-ft Hidden Valley monolith, **varied aspect** (all sides); the hub of J-Tree. `steep .5 · sun .7 · drain .9 · lagH 0 · aspect varied`
- **Hemingway Buttress** — SW-facing roadside cracks, sun-drenched and grippy; warm on a clear winter day, an oven in summer. `steep .55 · sun .8 · drain .9 · lagH 0 · aspect SW`
- **Echo Rock** — low-angle slabs plus a shady North Wall, **varied aspect**; friction best when cold and crisp. `steep .4 · sun .65 · drain .9 · lagH 0 · aspect varied`

### Lake Tahoe — Granite, South Tahoe / Echo Summit, CA (38.7950, −120.1080)
Sierra granite along the Echo Summit corridor; the marquee crags are south-facing and sunny, so they're in season much of the year and dry fast.
- **Lover's Leap** — 600-ft S-facing dike wall above Strawberry; horizontal dikes give holds on otherwise-blank granite. `steep .5 · sun .75 · drain .5 · lagH 2 · aspect S`
- **Sugarloaf** — 400-ft S-facing spire above Kyburz, low and sun-baked — the winter / snowed-in-elsewhere pick. `steep .55 · sun .85 · drain .6 · lagH 1 · aspect S`
- **Phantom Spires** — exposed SW-facing granite spires, great late fall to early summer; sunny and quick to dry but windy. `steep .5 · sun .75 · drain .6 · lagH 1 · aspect SW`

### Bishop — Granite & welded tuff, Eastern Sierra, CA (37.4200, −118.5800)
Owens Valley high desert: bone-dry and sunny, so rock comes back fast after the rare rain. Mixed rock — sport tuff in the gorge, sharp granite on the bench. Cold winter mornings, hot summers; spring/fall prime.
- **Owens River Gorge** — welded-tuff sport mecca with both sunny and shady walls (**varied aspect**); pick a side by season. `steep .6 · sun .6 · drain .8 · lagH 0 · aspect varied`
- **The Buttermilks** — sharp quartz-monzonite highballs, **varied aspect**; grips best cold and dry, friction fades as it warms. `steep .6 · sun .7 · drain .9 · lagH 0 · aspect varied`
- **Pine Creek Canyon** — granite sport/trad higher up a canyon, cooler summer escape; a mix of sun and shade, dries well. `steep .55 · sun .65 · drain .6 · lagH 1 · aspect S`

### The Needles — Granite, Southern Sierra, CA (36.1156, −118.4989)
Remote, flawless granite spires high over the Kern River (~2,400 m). South-facing and sunny, summer-only (snowed-in road otherwise); exposed, so wind and afternoon thunderstorms are the real hazard. Thin dry air dries it fast.
- **The Warlock** — the crowning tower; the S Face is the longest route (7–8 pitches). `steep .6 · sun .7 · drain .6 · lagH 3 · aspect S`
- **The Sorcerer** — SE-facing cirque testpiece (Thin Ice), morning sun and quick to dry. `steep .55 · sun .7 · drain .6 · lagH 3 · aspect SE`
- **The Wizard** — airy S-facing granite (Yellow Brick Road), sunny but committing and exposed. `steep .55 · sun .7 · drain .6 · lagH 3 · aspect S`

## Calibration guide (setting the numbers for a new crag)
- `drain`: basalt/rain-shadow ≈ 0.9–0.95; well-featured granite ≈ 0.35–0.6; seepy valley-floor granite ≈ 0.25–0.3; sandstone would be lower still (and don't climb it wet).
- `steep`: slab ≈ 0.15–0.25; vertical ≈ 0.45–0.6; overhung "climbs in the rain" ≈ 0.85–0.95.
- `sun`: deep shade ≈ 0.35–0.45; mixed ≈ 0.6–0.7; open south/southwest ≈ 0.85–0.95.
- `lagH`: roadside low-elevation ≈ 0–1; standard approach ≈ 2–3; high/alpine-ish ≈ 5+.

## Tick-list tiers (how conditions map to what to climb)
- **Marginal / just after rain / high dew point:** steep refuges and fast-draining rock — the Cheeks, Exit 38 Far Side, Vantage and Trout Creek basalt, Lookout Point slabs (dry fast, warm slow).
- **Decent day, sun's been on the wall:** steep-to-vertical crack lines that shed water — LTW central lines, Chief Grand Wall, Smith's Dihedrals.
- **Prime, cold, stable, low dew point only:** the seepy slabs and friction testpieces — Great Northern Slab, the Apron, Broughton's Red Wall, thin face and tips cracks.
- **Prime + committing:** higher/multipitch that lags after weather — the Upper Town Wall (Town Crier, Green Dragon), big Chief routes.
